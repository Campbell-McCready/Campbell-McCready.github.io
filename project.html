Drugs use in the UK

Before analysing the characteristics and costs, I will look at the main drugs that are used in the UK and the regions in which they are most prevalent. The chart below shows the estimated number of users of different illegal substances (excluding cannabis) between 2010 and 2021. The slider can be used to look at how the data differs from across these years and hovering over each bar will highlight the data:

We can see that cocaine is consistently the second most consumed illegal substance, with other class A’s like hallucinogens and ecstasy not even reaching half the amount of consumption. Cannabis was excluded from this chart because its consumption was three times the average of cocaine, and many multiples higher than any of the other drugs. Along with the number of drugs used each year we can look at which regions use the most. Below is a choropleth map showing the proportion of the population that consumed illegal substances in 2023 in each region of England and Wales:

We can see that the South-West has the highest the highest proportion of drug use. This interested me, why would the South-West have a higher proportion of drug use than London, for example. After doing some research I discovered that a likely reason for this is due to ‘county lines’. This is a model of drug distribution in which metropolitan organised crime groups sell drugs in provincial areas. Because Cornwall, Devon and Somerset are some of the most rural counties in the UK, they’re ideal candidates for these tactics. Coastal towns with harbours such as Plymouth or Penzance are also vulnerable to overseas drug smuggling.
